# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Municipal decision-makers and department staff: Zoning Administrators, Planning Directors, Building Officials, and City Managers at mid-size cities (50K–300K population). They are time-pressured, accountable to the public, and skeptical of technology that overpromises. They open this site on a laptop, often during a workday, looking for a credible solution to a real operational problem — permit backlogs, inconsistent reviews, overwhelmed staff. They need to trust before they demo.

Secondary: Real estate developers and independent landlords (future audience as Site Genie and Rent Genie come online). They are results-oriented and impatient with friction.

## Product Purpose

ASAP is a platform of AI-powered "Genie" products built on spatial intelligence. Each product solves a specific pain point in the intersection of land, permits, and property:

- **Zone Genie** (flagship, live): Automates zoning and permit review for municipal planning departments. Reduces review time from weeks to minutes.
- **Site Genie** (pipeline): AI-powered site selection and development feasibility for real estate developers.
- **Fence Genie** (pipeline): Homeowner-facing fence permit and site plan generator.

The ASAP website is the platform brand home — it leads with Zone Genie to establish credibility, hints at the growing suite, and converts qualified visitors into demo requests. Success = a planning director filling out the form.

## Brand Personality

Approachable, Modern, Civic.

Voice is confident but never arrogant. Clear without being clinical. It talks to smart people who are busy — no jargon, no fluff. Tone sits between a trusted consultant and a well-designed tool: expert, warm, direct.

The brand should feel like a modern SaaS company that genuinely understands local government — not a govtech vendor doing SaaS cosplay, and not a government portal trying to look modern.

## References

- **x.ai** — Dark-first, editorial confidence. Large type does the work. No decorative fills, no gradient cards. The product's authority speaks without ornament. This is the tonal target: serious, modern, unhurried.

## Anti-references

- **No generic govtech** — sites that lead with stock photos of city halls, blue gradients, and "citizen-centric solutions." Slow, institutional, forgettable.
- **No SaaS-cream AI startups** — the off-white/beige + rounded cards + purple accent look. Overused, undifferentiated.
- **No aggressive dashboard screenshots** — cluttered UI fills that try to prove sophistication through complexity.
- **No hero-metric templates** — big number, small label, supporting stat. SaaS cliché, especially dangerous for a platform targeting government trust.

## Design Principles

1. **Trust through clarity.** Municipal buyers are risk-averse. Every design choice should reduce cognitive load and build confidence — clean hierarchy, plain language, no visual noise that obscures the value proposition.
2. **Civic without institutional.** The brand lives in government contexts but should feel like the best modern software those contexts have ever seen. Think: what if a great SaaS studio designed for a city?
3. **The suite is the story.** Zone Genie is the entry point, but ASAP's value compounds as the suite grows. Design should hint at a platform, not a single product.
4. **Show restraint, earn attention.** Decorative choices should be deliberate and few. When something has visual weight, it means something.
5. **Earn the demo.** Every section exists to move a skeptical, busy decision-maker one step closer to the form. No section should exist purely for decoration.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA minimum across all text, interactive elements, and color contrast. Respect prefers-reduced-motion. Avoid relying solely on color to convey meaning. All form fields labeled, all images with alt text.
